HSL Transitlog Usability Evaluation

HSL Transitlog is a web application developed by HSL. It is a product for the purpose of tracking and monitoring the public transportation schedules in the whole Helsinki region. Additionally, it also provides functions for locating problems related to traffic control.

The main interface is a screen with a map, a top panel, and a sidebar. The top panel is to filter dates, bus lines, stops and even specific vehicle numbers. It can show both real-time and archived public transport journeys.
At present, this system only provides the data view of trains, trams, and buses. The metro will also be included in the system in the future. The languages supported by the system are Finnish, Swedish and English.

Customer’s Wishes

In general, the project manager expected us to evaluate the usability of the system and give constructive suggestions to improve the system and its UI. They also suggested us to improve the onboarding/training process. The Transitlog project is going to be released in May 2019. Thus, it is crucial for the client to detect major usability problems before it is actually put into use.

A first meeting with our client helped us understand their hopes and expectations. They specified user groups, of which the most important were contract handler, helpdesk officer, and feedback manager.

Application Evaluation Focus

After understanding the customer’s wishes, we learnt the usage of the system and performed initial quick evaluations within our team. We explored the potential use-cases we had learned and gathered our first impressions of the usability without keeping strict guidelines in mind. From those evaluations, we concluded that the system had no critical issues. However, actions used to filter search fields and achieve the required system state needed further investigation and evaluation. This helped the customer understand how to improve the system.

Research Questions

Expert Evaluation
The focus of our expert evaluation was on measuring the effectiveness, efficiency and satisfaction of the system. The focus was broad because we wanted to cover as many issues as possible to start with. We used cognitive walkthrough and heuristic evaluations to answer the following research questions:

  • Gulf of Evaluation: Does the system respond as users expect?
  • Gulf of Execution: Does the system allow users to do what they need to do?

However, this focus was very broad. We should not give the equal importance to these 3 usability characteristics. The research questions doesn’t align with 3-characteristic scope.

Empirical Evaluation
The focus during the empirical evaluation was on the effectiveness and efficiency of the journey filtering system of Transitlog for different user groups. Compared to the previous research focus, we narrowed down the scope focusing more on the common task of main users.

To act on this focus, we formulated the following general research questions.

Yes/No

  • RQ1 [Effectiveness] Are the users able to complete their tasks?
  • RQ2 [Effectiveness] Do the users understand the color coding of the application?

Close-ended questions

  • RQ3 [Efficiency] What journey filtering mechanisms are most used?
  • RQ4 [Efficiency] How many steps does it take to recover from a wrong result?

Open-ended questions

  • RQ5 [Efficiency] How efficiently do the users find the bus and the stop that they are looking for?

  • RQ6 [Satisfaction] What is the general disposition toward Transitlog?

Methods

Expert evaluation
We applied “10 usability heuristic for user interface design”. Also, each of us did a cognitive walkthrough with specific scenarios for the user groups as follows.

  • Contract handlers: they are responsible to check faults in routes
  • Feedback managers: they verify and take care of written feedback
  • Helpdesk officer: they offer assistance through calls

Empirical evaluation
We requested our customers to set up interviews with participants for our 3 different user groups namely: contract handler, helpdesk officer and feedback manager. Our customers swiftly accepted our request and set up half an hour meetings with representatives from each group.

The usability test was carried out in an HSL meeting room. Participant, facilitator, data logger and observer all stayed in the same room, as the picture shown below. The data logger sat left behind the participant with the camera. The facilitator sat right next to the participant, while the test observer sat opposite to the participant so as to observe the participant’s facial expression.

Furthermore, we designed a common task and specific taksks for each user groups.

Findings

Expert Evaluations

  • Visibility of System Status
    The functions of the highlighted parts as shown below is not easy for the user to understand. Thus the state of the system is not obvious to the user making it difficult for the users to understand the current status of the system.

  • Match between The System and The Real World
    Terms such as “live” is confusing as the functionality is actually “playback”. Similar terms and jargon are used although they do not follow usual conventions.

  • Consistency and Standards
    There are some inconsistencies such as the inconsistencies in translation and word selection. Some of the buttons do not function in consistency with what they are supposed to.

  • Flexibility and Efficiency of Use
    The loading functionality does not have clear messages and the placement of buttons should be reconsidered.

  • Aesthetic and Minimalist Design
    The icons used in the system are unclear. And, the side panels are not adjustable, so it will be problematic for a smaller screen eg. mobile screen.

Empirical Evaluations
In general, two out of five participants were able to complete all tasks, and one participant failed all tasks. In terms of color coding used in the system to represent the various states of the transport being analyzed, only one participant completely understood the meaning directly, the rest were not sure or had to think a while about the meaning of the colors.

Each user group had different filtering habits, but the most used filter was on the bus line, route direction and date which are placed in the top search bar (see Figure 9). However, none of the participants was able to recover from a wrong result, they simply reset all inputs and started over again.

Participants performed tasks differently according to their own technical experience and mental state. We have listed the specific insights from the tests in Appendix B, but we summarize them here. Performance was varied, there was one participant who succeeded in completing each task with less than 10 actions in 2 minutes. Another participant was too stressed to calm down and accomplish tasks.
